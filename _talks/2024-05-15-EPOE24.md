Abstract:
<!-- Terrestrial planets, including the Earth, were bombarded by impactors, some even following the early giant impact phase of planet formation. For Earth, these impacts delivered the highly sidereophile elements excess seen in Earth’s mantle. The late delivery of small to mid-sized impactors (100m– 1km) also provides a route to deliver the feedstock molecules important for prebiotic chemistry and the origin of life. In this work, we use dynamical models to predict the total accreted mass during the Late Veneer, taking into account the buoyancy of metallic iron in a post-rheological transition mantle. We find that only metallic diapirs smaller than about 10m in diameter were entrained in Earth’s mantle, while larger diapirs sank and directly merged with the Earth’s core. A number of origins scenaros invoke multiple impacts in the same location to deliver a prebiotic inventory, and reactivate prebiotic chemistry. We use a Monte Carlo model to calculate the number of overlapping craters on the early-Earth, and find these scenarios are constrained to early times (>3.9Gyr), and require the formation of ferrocyanide salts with very long (i.e >1000yrs) degradation timescales. Successful cometary delivery requires very low impact velocities (<15km/s) to prevent the degradation of prebiotic molecules. We show that such impacts are most likely onto low-mass exoplanets in tightly-packed planetary systems, orbiting high-mass stars.  -->
